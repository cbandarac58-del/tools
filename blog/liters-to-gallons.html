Liters to Gallons Conversion - US Gallon vs UK Imperial Gallon Guide

Published: July 21, 2026 · 5 min read · SmartToolzAI

Converting liquid volume can be one of the trickiest calculations to perform, largely because not all gallons are created equal. When someone asks you to convert liters to gallons, your very first question should be: "Which gallon?" Historically, volume measurements have varied wildly from country to country. Today, the world primarily deals with the metric liter, the US liquid gallon, and the UK Imperial gallon.

In this guide, we will break down the differences between these measurements, provide you with the exact formulas you need, and offer easy-to-read charts for both US and UK conversions. Whether you are brewing beer, calculating fuel economy, or just following an international recipe, we've got you covered.

The Formula

The calculation you use depends entirely on where you are located or the origin of the data you are converting. The US liquid gallon is smaller than the UK Imperial gallon.

US Gallon Conversion

In the United States, one gallon is defined as exactly 231 cubic inches, which equates to roughly 3.785 liters.

1 US gal = 3.78541 L

L = US gal × 3.78541
US gal = L ÷ 3.78541

UK Imperial Gallon Conversion

In the United Kingdom, Canada, and some Caribbean nations, the Imperial gallon is used. This gallon is significantly larger, defined as exactly 4.54609 liters.

1 UK gal = 4.54609 L

L = UK gal × 4.54609
UK gal = L ÷ 4.54609

Fuel Economy - MPG vs L/100km

One of the most common reasons people convert volume is to understand vehicle fuel efficiency. The US and UK measure fuel economy in Miles Per Gallon (MPG), but because their gallons are different sizes, a car getting 40 MPG in the UK is actually less efficient than a car getting 40 MPG in the US!

The rest of the world uses Liters per 100 kilometers (L/100km). This metric system measures fuel consumption rather than fuel economy—meaning a lower number is better. Converting between MPG and L/100km requires converting both the distance (miles to km) and the volume (gallons to liters) simultaneously, which is why having a firm grasp on the liter-to-gallon ratio is so important.

Common Uses and Examples

Let's take a look at how these numbers play out in our day-to-day lives:

  • Car Gas Tanks: A standard mid-sized sedan outside the US might have a 50-liter fuel tank. If you import that car to the US, you can expect it to hold about 13.2 US gallons of gasoline.
  • Beverages: Large soda bottles are famously sold in 2-liter sizes around the world. A 2-liter bottle is just over half a US gallon (0.53 gal).
  • Homebrewing and Recipes: When following a European soup or beer recipe calling for 20 liters of liquid, an American brewer would need to prepare equipment capable of holding at least 5.3 US gallons.

Always double-check the origin of your recipe or manual. Assuming a UK gallon when the author meant a US gallon can result in a 20% error in your final product!
