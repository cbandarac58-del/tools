Celsius to Fahrenheit Conversion - Formula, Chart and Examples

Published: July 21, 2026 · 6 min read · SmartToolzAI

Temperature affects almost every aspect of our daily lives, from what we wear when stepping outside to how we bake our favorite desserts. However, the world is split between two major temperature scales: Celsius and Fahrenheit. Understanding how to convert between these two systems is incredibly useful for travelers, scientists, cooks, and anyone consuming international media.

While the Fahrenheit scale is firmly entrenched in the United States, the Celsius scale (formerly known as centigrade) is the standard for the vast majority of the global population. In this comprehensive guide, we will explore the mathematical formula used to convert Celsius to Fahrenheit, provide a step-by-step example, list key reference temperatures, and explain the historical context of these widely used scales.

The Formula

Unlike simple distance conversions where you just multiply by a single factor, temperature conversion requires a two-step mathematical process. Because the two scales have different starting points (zero points) and different sized degrees, the formula involves both multiplication and addition.

F = (C × 9/5) + 32

Or, using decimals:
F = (C × 1.8) + 32

If you need to go in the reverse direction and convert Fahrenheit back to Celsius, you must subtract 32 first, and then multiply by the fraction 5/9.

C = (F - 32) × 5/9

Or, using decimals:
C = (F - 32) ÷ 1.8

Step-by-Step Example

Let's walk through a practical example using normal human body temperature, which is widely recognized as 37°C in most parts of the world. How do we convert this to the familiar Fahrenheit scale?

  1. Start with the Celsius temperature: 37
  2. Multiply by 9/5 (or 1.8): 37 × 1.8 = 66.6
  3. Add 32 to the result: 66.6 + 32 = 98.6

The result is 98.6°F, which is the standard reading for a healthy human body temperature in the United States.

Key Reference Temperatures

Memorizing a few anchor points can help you quickly gauge how hot or cold it is without doing any math. Here are the most important reference temperatures to know:

  • Water Freezes: The Celsius scale was designed so that water freezes at exactly 0°C. In Fahrenheit, this occurs at 32°F.
  • Room Temperature: A comfortable indoor setting is typically around 20°C to 22°C, which corresponds to 68°F to 72°F.
  • Body Temperature: As calculated above, 37°C is the equivalent of 98.6°F.
  • Water Boils: At sea level, water boils at a perfectly round 100°C, which equates to 212°F.
  • The Intersection Point: There is one unique temperature where both scales read exactly the same number. At -40 degrees, Celsius and Fahrenheit are equal (-40°C = -40°F).
  • Oven Temperatures: If you are following a European recipe, a standard baking temperature of 180°C translates to roughly 350°F to 356°F.

Celsius to Kelvin

In the realm of science, the Kelvin scale is often used instead of Celsius or Fahrenheit. Kelvin is an absolute thermodynamic temperature scale that uses absolute zero as its null point. Converting Celsius to Kelvin is much simpler than converting to Fahrenheit.

K = C + 273.15

For example, water freezes at 273.15 K and boils at 373.15 K.

Which Countries Use Fahrenheit?

If you're wondering why we still need these conversions, it's largely because the United States continues to use the Fahrenheit scale for everyday applications like weather forecasts, cooking, and medicine. Alongside the US, a very small handful of other nations and territories also use Fahrenheit, including the Bahamas, the Cayman Islands, Liberia, Palau, the Federated States of Micronesia, and the Marshall Islands. The rest of the world has officially adopted the Celsius scale.
